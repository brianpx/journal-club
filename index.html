Purpose

The Journal Club aims to enhance residents' skills in critically appraising medical literature. By the session's conclusion, participants will be equipped to apply evidence-based insights to clinical practice effectively.

Topic

Spinal versus General Anesthesia in Hip Surgery: Outcomes and Perioperative Care

Objectives

  1. Develop critical appraisal skills using a structured checklist (validity, results, applicability).
  2. Interpret effect sizes and confidence intervals, moving beyond reliance on p-values.
  3. Apply research findings to clinical vignettes relevant to anesthesia practice.
  4. Understand limitations of generalizing fracture trial data to elective THA, where ERAS pathways and regional techniques are standard.

Critical Appraisal Checklist

  1. Are the results valid? (Internal Validity)

    • ☐ Was randomization adequate?
    • ☐ Was allocation concealed?
    • ☐ Who was blinded (if anyone)?
    • ☐ Were baseline characteristics similar?
    • ☐ Was ITT analysis performed?
    • ☐ Were crossovers or missing data significant?

  2. What were the results? (Magnitude & Precision)

    • ☐ Effect sizes: HR, OR, CI, ARR, NNT/NNH?
    • ☐ Were results clinically meaningful or only statistically significant?
    • ☐ Were harms/adverse effects reported?
    • ☐ Any time-course patterns (early vs late outcomes)?

  3. Will these results help my patients? (Applicability)

    • ☐ Does the study population match our patients?
    • ☐ Are the outcomes patient-centered (survival, ambulation, pain, satisfaction)?
    • ☐ Any barriers in our practice (anticoagulation, resources, PACU staffing)?
    • ☐ How might local ERAS/THA pathways differ?

A Tale of Two Patient Groups: Emergency Fracture vs. Elective Replacement

Hip Fracture Surgery

Key Findings from the REGAIN Trials

=

No difference in 1-year survival or ability to walk between Spinal and General Anesthesia.

Spinal Anesthesia was associated with slightly more pain on the first day after surgery.

Spinal Anesthesia led to higher prescription analgesic use at 60 days post-op.

Patient satisfaction was similar between both anesthesia types.

Conclusion: For emergency hip fracture surgery in elderly patients, both anesthesia types lead to similar major long-term outcomes.

Elective Hip Replacement

ERAS Best Practice Recommendations

Neuraxial Anesthesia (e.g., Spinal) is the recommended standard of care as part of an Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) pathway.

General Anesthesia alone is explicitly NOT considered best practice for planned replacements.

Anesthesia is combined with multimodal analgesia to optimize pain control and speed up recovery.

Conclusion: For planned hip replacements, neuraxial anesthesia is preferred to improve recovery and patient outcomes.

The Bottom Line: Different Patients, Different Strategies

The key takeaway is that evidence from emergency hip fracture trials should not be generalized to elective hip replacement patients. While major outcomes are similar for fracture surgery regardless of anesthesia type, neuraxial anesthesia remains the best practice for planned procedures to enhance recovery and align with modern care pathways.
