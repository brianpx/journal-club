Intraoperative Anesthetic Depth & Postoperative Delirium

Examining the evidence: Does EEG-guided anesthetic management reduce postoperative delirium in older surgical patients?

The Central Question

If EEG burst suppression during anesthesia is associated with postoperative delirium, why doesn't EEG-guided management consistently prevent it?

Consider: Is deep anesthesia a cause of delirium, or merely a marker of vulnerability?

Why This Matters to Anesthesiologists

10–70%

POD incidence in older surgical patients, varying by risk factors and surgery type

2–3×

Increased mortality risk, longer ICU/hospital stays, and higher healthcare costs

35–40%

Of delirium cases may be preventable with targeted interventions

As the anesthesiologist, you control key modifiable factors:

Anesthetic depth Drug selection Hemodynamic management Opioid dosing EEG monitoring decisions

Reconciling the Results: Why Do These Trials Differ?

1

Different Research Questions

Evered

Does deliberately targeting light vs deep anesthesia affect delirium?

ENGAGES

Does giving clinicians EEG feedback reduce delirium vs usual care?

2

Magnitude of Separation Achieved

Evered achieved substantially larger differences in anesthetic exposure:

  • Evered: Δ0.20 MAC, Δ13 BIS points
  • ENGAGES: Δ0.11 MAC only
3

Delirium is Multifactorial

Even “perfect” hypnotic titration cannot overcome all risk factors:

  • Baseline frailty
  • Cognitive reserve
  • Surgical inflammation
  • Pain/opioids
  • Sleep disruption
  • Postoperative complications
4

Hemodynamic Confounding in Evered

The light anesthesia group had higher MAP (+4 mmHg) and used fewer vasopressors (−20%). This could either:

Mediate

Lighter → better perfusion → less delirium

Confound

Hemodynamics, not depth, drive the benefit

Strengths

  • True dose-separation RCT design
  • Rigorous twice-daily delirium assessment
  • Mechanistic linkage (burst suppression)

ENGAGES — Deep Dive

Population

  • Adults ≥60 years
  • Major surgery with GA
  • Excluded if delirious at baseline
  • Single-center (Barnes-Jewish)

Intervention

EEG-guided anesthesia — clinicians encouraged to reduce volatile to minimize EEG suppression and BIS <40

Comparator

Usual care with EEG waveforms hidden (signal quality visible only)

Outcomes

  • Primary: Delirium POD 1–5
  • Volatile concentration, EEG suppression
  • Hypotension (MAP <60)
  • Movement, awareness, complications

The Null Result

26% vs 23% delirium (Guided vs Usual). Despite lower MAC (0.69 vs 0.80) and less EEG suppression (7 vs 13 min), EEG-guided care did not significantly reduce delirium.

Clinical Tradeoff Signal

More undesirable intraoperative movement in guided group: 22.3% vs 15.4%

Strengths

  • Large RCT (n≈1232), 98.5% follow-up
  • Pragmatic design, many clinicians
  • Robust delirium detection (CAM + chart)
  • Confirmed physiologic separation

Limitations

  • Modest separation (Δ0.11 MAC, Δ6 min)
  • Contamination: some controls saw EEG
  • Co-intervention (safety bundle) in both arms
  • Single center — generalizability

Knowledge Check

1 In the Evered et al. study, what were the BIS targets for the two groups?
BIS 35 (deep) vs BIS 50 (light). The study randomized patients to either a “light” target of BIS 50 or a “deep” target of BIS 35, achieving a true dose-separation design.
2 What was the primary outcome finding in the Evered et al. study?
Lower delirium in the BIS 50 group: 19% vs 28% (OR 0.58, p=0.010).
3 Which potential confounding factor was identified in Evered et al.?
Higher MAP (~4 mmHg) and lower vasopressor use (~20%) in the BIS 50 group, which could independently influence delirium risk.
4 What longer-term cognitive outcome was noted in the Evered et al. study?
Fewer patients with AMTS ≤6 at 1 year in the BIS 50 group (9% vs 20%), suggesting potential longer-term cognitive benefit.
5 What was the intervention in the ENGAGES trial?
EEG-guided anesthesia with clinicians encouraged to minimize EEG suppression and avoid BIS <40, compared to usual care where EEG was hidden.
6 What was the primary outcome result in ENGAGES?
No significant difference: 26.0% delirium in EEG-guided vs 23.0% in usual care (p=0.22), despite achieving physiologic separation.
7 What clinical tradeoff was observed in the EEG-guided group of ENGAGES?
More undesirable intraoperative movement: 22.3% vs 15.4%, reflecting the challenge of attempting lighter anesthesia.
8 What was the magnitude of volatile concentration difference achieved in ENGAGES?
0.69 vs 0.80 MAC (Δ0.11 MAC) — a modest but real difference.
9 Why did Evered show benefit but ENGAGES did not?
Evered tested a larger dose separation (BIS 35 vs 50) and asked a fundamentally different question (targeted depth vs EEG feedback). Different questions with different achieved separations.
10 Based on both studies, what is the most evidence-based practice recommendation?
Avoiding prolonged burst suppression may be reasonable in high-risk patients, though depth alone may not prevent delirium. Delirium is multifactorial.
