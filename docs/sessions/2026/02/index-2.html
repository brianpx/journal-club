GLP-1 Receptor Agonists & Perioperative Aspiration Risk

Examining the evidence: Do GLP-1 RAs increase aspiration pneumonia risk, and should we hold them before procedures?

Faculty: Dr. Artin Yeranossian, Dr. Gloria Belonwu

Resident Presenters: Dr. Nick Balardi, Dr. Ravina Brring

The Central Question

If GLP-1 receptor agonists delay gastric emptying, why do studies show conflicting results on aspiration pneumonia risk?

Consider: Is delayed gastric emptying a clinically meaningful cause of aspiration, or merely a physiologic finding without proportionate clinical consequences?

Why This Matters to Anesthesiologists

6–15%

US adults now prescribed GLP-1 RAs

4–5×

Increase in GLP-1 RA prescriptions since 2020

0.6–0.8%

Baseline aspiration pneumonia rate in surgical patients

As the anesthesiologist, you control key modifiable factors:

Preoperative fasting duration GLP-1 RA hold timing Procedure type selection Sedation depth/airway protection Gastric ultrasound utilization

Reconciling the Results

1

Different Procedure Populations

Yeo

Endoscopy-specific: Upper airway manipulation, stomach insufflation, supine positioning — all increase aspiration opportunity

Chen

Mixed surgical cohort: Many procedures (knee/hip arthroplasty, hernia repair) have inherently low aspiration risk regardless of gastric contents

2

Exposure Definition Differences

  • Yeo: Chronic users (≥6 months, 2 refills) — more likely true steady-state delayed gastric emptying
  • Chen: Any prescription within 30 days — includes new starts, may miss actual medication use
3

The Upper GI Hypothesis

Yeo's subgroup analysis reveals the signal:

  • Upper endoscopy alone: HR 1.48 (p = 0.018)
  • Combined upper + lower: HR 2.26 (p = 0.007)
  • Colonoscopy alone: HR 0.56 (p = 0.16) — NO increased risk
  • Suggests stomach manipulation/insufflation is the key risk modifier
4

Propofol as Effect Modifier

Yeo found propofol sedation amplified the risk signal:

With Propofol

HR 1.49 (95% CI 1.08–2.06, p = 0.014)

Without Propofol

HR 1.31 (95% CI 0.78–2.20, p = 0.31)

5

Absolute Risk Context

Even with statistically significant findings, absolute risks remain low:

  • Yeo: 0.83% vs 0.63% = NNH ~500
  • Chen: 0.7% vs 0.6% = No significant difference
  • Clinical significance vs statistical significance

Yeo et al. — Deep Dive

Population

  • Age 21–70 years
  • Upper and/or lower endoscopy
  • January 2018 – December 2020
  • Excluded: Prior esophagectomy, gastrectomy, fundoplication
  • Excluded: Intubation/tracheostomy within 6 months

Intervention

GLP-1RA use ≥6 months with ≥2 refills within 6 months before procedure

Comparator

Never used GLP-1RA during study period

Outcomes

  • Primary: Aspiration pneumonia within 30 days
  • Subgroups: Sex, propofol use, endoscopy type

Key Results

1.33

Adjusted HR overall (p = 0.036)

1.49

HR with propofol (p = 0.014)

2.26

HR combined upper/lower (p = 0.007)

Strengths

  • Large matched cohort (15,000+ per group after PSM)
  • Rigorous 59-covariate propensity score matching
  • Pre-ASA guidance era (no hold contamination)
  • Clinically relevant subgroup analyses
  • Chronic user definition increases biological plausibility

Limitations

  • Retrospective claims data — cannot confirm actual medication adherence
  • No data on fasting duration or gastric ultrasound
  • ICD-10 coding variability for aspiration pneumonia
  • Cannot distinguish aspiration pneumonitis vs pneumonia
  • No information on GLP-1RA hold timing
  • Three covariates with minor residual imbalance (SMD <0.25)

Chen et al. — Deep Dive

Population

  • Age ≥18 years
  • 14 common US surgical procedures
  • April 2020 – September 2022
  • Excluded: Multiple surgeries, pneumonia/resp failure within 90 days
  • Required: 90-day continuous insurance enrollment

Intervention

Any GLP-1RA prescription within 30 days before surgery (dulaglutide, exenatide, liraglutide, semaglutide)

Comparator

No GLP-1RA prescription

Outcomes

  • Primary: 30-day aspiration pneumonia
  • Secondary: 90-day acute respiratory failure

The Null Result

0.7% vs 0.6% aspiration pneumonia (GLP-1RA vs non-users). Despite higher baseline comorbidity burden in GLP-1RA users, no significant difference after adjustment.

Robust Across Sensitivity Analyses

Five sensitivity analyses (surgery type adjustment, anesthesia type, indication restriction, death exclusion, propensity score) all showed consistent null results

Strengths

  • Large cohort (366,476 patients)
  • Nationwide commercial claims — multi-institutional
  • 14 representative surgical procedures
  • Multiple sensitivity analyses
  • Pre-ASA guidance era
  • Adjusted for extensive comorbidities

Limitations

  • Prescription ≠ actual medication use
  • 30-day exposure window may miss chronic users
  • Heterogeneous surgical population dilutes signal
  • No Medicare patients (younger, healthier cohort)
  • No granularity on fasting, gastric ultrasound, case cancellations
  • Population-level surgical acuity categorization

Knowledge Check

1 What was the primary finding of the Yeo et al. (Gastroenterology 2024) study?
GLP-1RA users had significantly higher aspiration pneumonia risk (HR 1.33, p = 0.036)
2 What was the primary finding of the Chen et al. (JAMA 2025) study?
No significant difference in aspiration pneumonia (OR 0.78, p = 0.12)
3 In which procedure subgroup did Yeo et al. find the highest aspiration risk?
Combined upper and lower endoscopy (HR 2.26, p = 0.007)
4 What was the effect of propofol sedation on aspiration risk in the Yeo study?
Propofol amplified risk (HR 1.49, p = 0.014) vs no significance without propofol
5 How did the two studies define GLP-1RA exposure differently?
Yeo required ≥6 months use + 2 refills; Chen used any prescription within 30 days
6 What is the current ASA guidance for weekly GLP-1 RAs before elective surgery?
Hold for 7 days before the procedure
7 Why might colonoscopy alone show no increased aspiration risk with GLP-1RAs?
No upper airway manipulation or gastric insufflation; stomach contents less relevant
8 What is a key limitation shared by both studies?
Neither could confirm actual medication adherence (prescription ≠ use)
9 What absolute aspiration pneumonia rates did both studies find?
0.6–0.8% in both groups — low absolute risk regardless of GLP-1RA status
10 How should these findings influence clinical practice?
Individualize based on procedure type (upper GI vs other), sedation plan, and patient symptoms; consider ASA guidance for high-risk scenarios
