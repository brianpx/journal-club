Intraoperative Anesthetic Depth & Postoperative Delirium

Examining the evidence: Does EEG-guided anesthetic management reduce postoperative delirium in older surgical patients?

The Central Question

If EEG burst suppression during anesthesia is associated with postoperative delirium, why doesn't EEG-guided management consistently prevent it?

Consider: Is deep anesthesia a cause of delirium, or merely a marker of vulnerability?

Why This Matters to Anesthesiologists

10–70%

POD incidence in older surgical patients

2–3×

Higher mortality, longer ICU/hospital stays

35–40%

May be preventable with targeted interventions

As the anesthesiologist, you control key modifiable factors:

Anesthetic depth Drug selection Hemodynamic management Opioid dosing EEG monitoring decisions

Reconciling the Results

1

Different Research Questions

Evered

Does deliberately targeting light vs deep anesthesia affect delirium?

ENGAGES

Does giving clinicians EEG feedback reduce delirium vs usual care?

2

Magnitude of Separation

  • Evered: Δ0.20 MAC, Δ13 BIS points
  • ENGAGES: Δ0.11 MAC
3

Delirium is Multifactorial

Even perfect hypnotic titration cannot overcome:

  • Baseline frailty
  • Cognitive reserve
  • Surgical inflammation
  • Pain/opioids
  • Sleep disruption
  • Postoperative complications
4

Hemodynamic Confounding (Evered)

Light anesthesia group had +4 mmHg higher MAP and −20% vasopressor use.

Mediate

Lighter anesthesia → better perfusion → less delirium

Confound

Hemodynamics, not depth, drive benefit

Evered et al. — Deep Dive

Population

  • Age ≥60
  • ASA 3–4
  • Major surgery ≥2 hours
  • Expected LOS ≥2 days
  • Baseline delirium excluded

Intervention

Volatile-based GA targeted to BIS ~50 (“light anesthesia”)

Comparator

Volatile-based GA targeted to BIS ~35 (“deep anesthesia”)

Outcomes

  • Primary: Delirium POD 1–5
  • MMSE (baseline/discharge)
  • AMTS at 30d and 1 year
  • Burst suppression time

Key Results

9%

Absolute Risk Reduction

+4

mmHg higher MAP (BIS 50)

20%

Less vasopressor use

Strengths

  • True dose-separation RCT design
  • Rigorous twice-daily delirium assessment
  • Mechanistic linkage (burst suppression)
  • 1-year cognitive follow-up

Limitations

  • Hemodynamic confounding
  • Substudy of BALANCED
  • AMTS screening tool only; no multiplicity adjustment
  • Limited awareness/movement tradeoff analysis

ENGAGES — Deep Dive

Population

  • Adults ≥60
  • Major surgery with GA
  • Excluded if delirious at baseline
  • Single-center (Barnes-Jewish)

Intervention

EEG-guided anesthesia — clinicians encouraged to reduce volatile to minimize EEG suppression and BIS <40

Comparator

Usual care with EEG waveforms hidden

Outcomes

  • Primary: Delirium POD 1–5
  • Volatile concentration
  • EEG suppression
  • Hypotension (MAP <60)
  • Movement, awareness, complications

The Null Result

26% vs 23% delirium (Guided vs Usual). Despite lower MAC (0.69 vs 0.80) and less EEG suppression (7 vs 13 min), no significant reduction in delirium.

Clinical Tradeoff Signal

More undesirable intraoperative movement in guided group: 22.3% vs 15.4%

Strengths

  • Large RCT (n ≈ 1232)
  • 98.5% follow-up
  • Pragmatic design
  • Robust delirium detection
  • Confirmed physiologic separation

Limitations

  • Modest separation (Δ0.11 MAC, Δ6 min)
  • Some control contamination
  • Co-intervention safety bundle
  • Single center — limited generalizability

Knowledge Check

1 What were the BIS targets in the Evered trial?
BIS 35 (deep) vs BIS 50 (light anesthesia)
2 What was the primary outcome of the Evered trial?
Lower delirium in BIS 50 group (19% vs 28%, p = 0.010)
3 What was the key confounder in the Evered trial?
Higher MAP and lower vasopressor use in BIS 50 group
4 What was the Evered trial's long-term cognitive outcome?
Fewer AMTS ≤6 at 1 year in BIS 50 group (9% vs 20%)
5 What was the ENGAGES intervention?
EEG-guided anesthesia encouraging reduced suppression
6 What was the ENGAGES primary result?
No significant difference (26% vs 23%, p = 0.22)
7 What was the clinical tradeoff in ENGAGES?
More intraoperative movement (22.3% vs 15.4%)
8 What was the MAC difference in ENGAGES?
0.69 vs 0.80 MAC (Δ0.11)
9 Why did the two studies produce different results?
Evered tested larger dose separation and asked a fundamentally different question (targeted depth vs EEG feedback)
10 What is the evidence-based recommendation?
Avoid prolonged burst suppression in high-risk patients; recognize delirium is multifactorial
